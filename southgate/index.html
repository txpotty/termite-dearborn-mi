Your Pest Management Partner: Recognizing the Signs of Termite Infestation

It can be easy to overlook the subtle signs of a termite problem until significant damage has occurred. Being aware of potential indicators can help you catch an infestation early and prevent extensive repairs. Here are some common signs that you might need professional termite control services in Southgate, MI:

Mud Tubes Present on Walls or Foundation

Termites, particularly subterranean termites, build mud tubes to create protected pathways between their underground colonies and their food source (your wood). These tubes are typically the width of a pencil and are commonly found on external or internal walls, foundations, or even on pipes and other structures. Discovering mud tubes is a strong indicator of active termite presence. They provide termites with the humidity and darkness they need to travel safely. If you see these, resist the urge to break them open immediately, as this can sometimes cause termites to simply reroute. It's best to have a professional inspect them.

Damaged or Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites eat wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer of wood or paint on the surface. This makes the wood sound hollow when tapped. If you notice areas of wood that appear warped, discolored, or blistered, it could be a sign of internal damage caused by termites. As they consume the cellulose, they create hollow galleries within the wooden structure. Testing suspicious areas by gently tapping or probing them can reveal whether the wood is compromised. Don't assume that intact-looking wood is safe; internal damage can be significant even if the exterior appears fine.

Discarded Termite Swarmer Wings

Swarming is a natural part of the termite life cycle, where reproductive termites (swarmers) leave the colony to establish new ones. These swarmers have wings, which they typically shed after finding a mate and a suitable location. Finding small piles of discarded wings, especially near windowsills, doors, and light sources, is a telltale sign of a nearby termite colony. Swarming usually occurs during specific times of the year depending on the termite species and environmental conditions. While the swarming event itself is often brief, the presence of discarded wings indicates that a colony is established in or near your home.

Termite Droppings (Frass)

Drywood termites, which live entirely within the wood they infest, create small exit holes and push out their fecal pellets, known as frass. This frass is typically the color of the wood they have been consuming and often resembles sawdust or tiny granular pellets. Finding small piles of frass, particularly beneath wooden structures, can indicate a drywood termite infestation. The appearance of frass can vary depending on the type of wood being consumed. While subterranean termites also produce frass, it is often mixed with soil and less noticeable than drywood termite frass.

Clicking Sounds within Walls

Some species of termites, particularly soldier termites, can make soft clicking or tapping sounds within infested wood. While not always audible to the untrained ear, these sounds can sometimes be detected in quiet environments and may indicate active termite movement within your walls. Soldier termites bang their heads against the wood to signal danger to the colony. These sounds can be faint and difficult to pinpoint without specialized equipment. If you suspect you hear unusual noises coming from your walls, it's worth having a professional investigate.

Shelter Tubes on Trees or Fences

Termites don't just infest homes; they can also damage outdoor structures like trees, fences, and sheds. Similar to the mud tubes on your foundation, finding shelter tubes extending up the trunks of nearby trees or on wooden fences can indicate a termite presence on your property. These tubes provide termites with protection from predators and the elements as they travel to and from their food source. These tubes can be harder to spot on rough tree bark but are a clear sign of termite activity on your landscape. Addressing termites on your property perimeter can help prevent them from reaching your home.
